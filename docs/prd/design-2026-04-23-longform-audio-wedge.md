## Problem Statement

Orpheus should not start as a broad "faceless YouTube AI video automation" product.

The first wedge is narrower: a product for full-time faceless educational explainer creators who publish script-heavy videos longer than 8 minutes, rely primarily on YouTube Partner Program revenue, and are blocked when long-form narration pipelines become unstable, too manual, or too expensive.

The immediate job to be done is not "make the whole video automatically." It is:

1. Take a long script.
2. Produce stable long-form narration without manual chunking.
3. Preserve upload frequency and narration quality so the creator can keep publishing revenue-generating videos.

## Demand Evidence

Current demand evidence is early but real:

1. Founder evidence is strong. The founder is a real YouTube creator making psychology and educational narration videos longer than 30 minutes and has already spent months building custom scheduling, batching, cooldowns, and async orchestration around `IndexTTS2` just to get reliable narration output.
2. External creator evidence exists. At least one other creator reported using `F5-TTS` on a paid cloud server because `ElevenLabs` is too expensive, and described a current workflow where a roughly 20,000-word script has to be manually split into about 8 chunks because longer runs are unstable.
3. The consequence is not cosmetic. If narration quality or reliability fails, the video does not get published. That reduces upload frequency, hurts `YPP` revenue, and can push creators away from this long-form content style entirely.
4. Subtitle instability is also painful, but current evidence does not yet prove that subtitles are the first purchase driver. The strongest proven pain is stable long-form audio output.

What is still missing:

1. Direct pricing evidence. No validated "yes, I will pay $X this month" commitment yet.
2. Broader sample size. Current evidence is founder plus a small number of adjacent creators, not a repeatable market proof set.
3. Unit economics. No locked model yet for cost to produce 20-30 minute stable output at a sustainable gross margin.

## Status Quo

Current users solve this problem through ugly workarounds:

1. Use `ElevenLabs`, but hit per-request text limits and expensive premium voice gating.
2. Self-host or rent cloud servers for `F5-TTS`, `IndexTTS2`, or similar systems.
3. Manually split long scripts into smaller sections to avoid failed runs.
4. Manually schedule runs, retries, cooldowns, and batching.
5. Search for voices in one tool, clone or recreate them elsewhere, then move back into the generation workflow.
6. Patch subtitle generation separately with tools like `CapCut` or `Gemini`, both of which are reported as unstable in real usage.

The real competitor is not "another AI SaaS." It is a stack of scripts, cloud GPUs, manual splitting, and human babysitting.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

### Target user

The first target user is:

`A full-time faceless educational explainer creator making script-heavy 8+ minute videos, earning primarily through YPP, and most afraid of narration instability causing lower publishing frequency and lower content quality.`

The most credible first subsegment is:

1. Knowledge explainers
2. Psychology / educational narration
3. Language / language-phenomena explainers

This is narrower than "faceless YouTube creators" and closer to the real production bottleneck discovered in this session.

### Narrowest wedge

The smallest version worth validating is:

`Long text -> stable audio`

Not:

1. Full video automation
2. Audio plus subtitles plus editing plus scene generation
3. A giant voice marketplace
4. A general-purpose TTS platform

If the product can reliably take a 20-30 minute script and output stable narration without manual chunking, it may already be valuable enough to test willingness to pay.

## Constraints

1. The product must solve a real publishing bottleneck, not just offer nicer TTS UX.
2. The first release should avoid bundling too many unstable promises together.
3. Pricing and unit economics are currently unknown and must be treated as validation questions, not assumptions.
4. The strongest user pain is stability and workflow relief, not brand story or voice abundance.
5. There is strong temptation to expand into "video + voice + script + visuals" automation immediately. That must be resisted in V1 validation.

## Premises

1. The correct first wedge is not generic faceless creators, but full-time YPP-driven educational explainers with long script-heavy videos.
2. The first buying reason is stable long-form audio generation, not automated visuals and not a large voice library.
3. The right immediate goal is to validate the smallest sellable result before completing a broader SaaS product definition.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Long-Form Audio Wedge

Summary: Sell one outcome only, stable long-form narration from long scripts without manual chunk splitting.

Effort: S
Risk: Low

Pros:

1. Closest to the strongest real pain demonstrated in this session.
2. Cleanest way to test willingness to pay.
3. Simplest path to calculating cost per successful long-form run.

Cons:

1. Not as visually impressive as a full creator workflow product.
2. Users will still ask for subtitles and downstream editing support.
3. Differentiation may feel weaker unless reliability is meaningfully better than the workaround stack.

Reuses:

1. Existing founder knowledge around `IndexTTS2`, batching, cooldowns, async runs, and manual scheduling pain.
2. Existing understanding of long-script failure modes.

### Approach B: Audio + SRT Creator Workflow

Summary: Deliver long-form narration plus usable subtitles as the first product.

Effort: M
Risk: Med

Pros:

1. More complete creator workflow value.
2. Easier to position against "just another TTS tool."
3. Closer to how creators actually finish publishing.

Cons:

1. Couples two hard reliability problems too early.
2. Subtitle instability can poison the whole validation loop.
3. Harder to know whether users are buying for audio, subtitles, or both.

Reuses:

1. Existing PRD thinking around subtitle export and billing semantics.
2. Existing founder awareness of subtitle tool failures.

### Approach C: Power-User Automation Wedge

Summary: Sell directly to advanced creators who already self-host or script their workflow and want orchestration relief.

Effort: S-M
Risk: Med

Pros:

1. Power users understand the value immediately.
2. Feedback quality will be sharp and technical.
3. Founder has direct empathy and credibility in this workflow.

Cons:

1. These users are also the most likely to build around the problem themselves.
2. Smaller market.
3. Risk of drifting into a tool for hackers instead of a real SaaS.

Reuses:

1. Founder's own workflow as reference implementation.
2. Existing automation experiments.

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach A.

Reason: it isolates the one thing currently backed by the strongest evidence, stable long-form narration, and gives the fastest path to validating pricing, unit economics, and repeat purchase intent. Approach B should only happen after Approach A proves users will pay. Approach C is useful later, but it is a riskier first customer definition because advanced users are more likely to self-solve.

## Open Questions

1. What monthly price, or per-minute price, feels obviously worth it to this wedge?
2. Can stable 20-30 minute outputs be delivered with acceptable failure rates and healthy gross margin?
3. Is subtitle export the second most valuable feature, or is local regeneration / voice flexibility more urgent?
4. Will ordinary non-technical creators trust a product that only promises audio first?
5. Should the early product use a small curated voice set or simply optimize for reliability first?

## Success Criteria

The wedge is validated if the founder can collect evidence that:

1. At least 5 creators in this exact wedge confirm the current workflow is painful in the same way.
2. At least 3 of them say they would pay for stable long-form narration if it removes manual splitting and babysitting.
3. At least 1-2 agree to a paid pilot or prepayment at a concrete price.
4. The product can complete representative long scripts with meaningfully better reliability than the current workaround.
5. Unit economics do not collapse at the intended usage pattern.

## Distribution Plan

This should start as a web product, not a package or developer tool.

Initial distribution:

1. Founder-led outreach to creators already in this wedge.
2. English landing page focused on one promise only: stable long-form narration for 8+ minute explainer creators.
3. Manual onboarding if needed for the first cohort.
4. Product demos based on real creator scripts, not generic marketing samples.

Near-term GTM:

1. Creator interviews and paid pilot sales before broad self-serve launch.
2. Publish example outputs for long-form educational explainers.
3. Validate whether "30-minute stable output" is the right lead promise or whether a different proof point converts better.

## Dependencies

1. A stable long-form audio generation path.
2. Cost model for successful long-form output.
3. A narrow English landing page and offer.
4. A concrete pricing hypothesis to test in interviews.
5. Real scripts and test cases from the intended wedge.

## The Assignment

Talk to 5 creators in this exact wedge and ask one concrete buying question:

`If I give you a tool that takes a long script and reliably outputs stable narration without manual chunking, what is the highest monthly price you would pay this month?`

Do not ask whether they "like the idea." Ask for a price and whether they would pay now.
